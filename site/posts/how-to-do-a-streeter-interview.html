The Streeter Interview: A Powerful Content Strategy You're Probably Overlooking

By Wade Paterson

What if the most compelling content for your audience was waiting for you right outside your front door — in the opinions and voices of everyday people? The streeter interview is a time-tested journalism technique that involves heading to a public place and asking random people a focused question. While it has deep roots in newsroom culture, it's also one of the most underused and adaptable content strategies for businesses, YouTubers, and creators of all kinds. Here's everything you need to know about how it works and how to do it well.

What Exactly Is a Streeter Interview?

The concept is beautifully simple. You go to a public place — a downtown sidewalk, a park, a busy plaza — and you ask random passersby a specific question on camera. In traditional journalism, reporters use streeter interviews to capture the public's pulse on current events or trending topics. But the format is incredibly versatile.

Whether you run a local business, manage a brand's social media presence, or create content around a niche hobby, you can adapt the streeter interview to serve your audience. The key is choosing the right questions — ones that will surface insights your viewers or customers actually care about.

A Real-World Example: Social Media and Real Estate

To illustrate how effective this technique can be, consider this example. When working as a social media and communications coordinator for RE/MAX of Western Canada, the goal was to help real estate agents understand how the public actually thinks about social media. Rather than relying on abstract data or industry reports, a streeter interview in downtown Kelowna, British Columbia, brought those insights to life through real voices.

The interview was designed to answer four specific questions:

  • Why do people use social media in the first place?
  • Will people follow businesses on social media?
  • How much time are people spending on social media each day?
  • Are there things businesses do on social media that people find annoying?

The responses were revealing. People primarily used social media to stay connected with far-flung friends and family. They followed local businesses — especially restaurants and small shops — to stay in the loop about what's happening in their community. Most spent about one to two hours a day scrolling. And their biggest pet peeve? Uninformed rants clogging up their feeds.

These candid, unscripted answers became a powerful training tool. Instead of lecturing real estate agents about social media best practices, the video let the public speak for itself — showing agents exactly what kind of content people want to engage with and what turns them off.

Tip #1: Don't Let Rejection Get You Down

Here's the reality of streeter interviews: most people will say no. Think about the last time someone interrupted you while you were going about your day and asked you to do something on camera. You probably weren't thrilled about it. That's the experience you'll be creating for many of the people you approach.

You might hear twenty people decline before one person agrees. That's completely normal. The important thing is to respect every "no" gracefully — thank the person, wish them a great day, and move on. Don't let it dampen your energy or your confidence. Stay friendly, stay kind, and keep asking.

The good news? You don't need a huge sample size. Even three or four willing participants can give you enough material for a short, engaging video with genuine variety in perspectives.

Tip #2: Prep Your Interviewee and Set the Context

Once someone agrees to participate, don't just launch into your questions. Take a moment to prepare them. Let them know:

  • How the video will be used (a business training event, a YouTube channel, a social media campaign, etc.)
  • Where the video will be shown or published
  • What types of questions you'll be asking and what information you're looking for

This isn't about scripting their answers — it's about making them feel comfortable and confident on camera. You want them to give you their best responses, not fumble through a curveball they weren't expecting. When people know the context, they relax, open up, and deliver far more authentic and useful content.

Tip #3: Invest in Decent Audio Equipment

This tip might seem technical, but it can make or break your streeter interview. When you're filming on a busy street, you're contending with cars, horns, crowds, wind, and all manner of unpredictable background noise. Your camera's built-in microphone is almost certainly not going to cut it.

Consider your audio equipment options:

  • A lavalier (lav) mic: Clips onto your interviewee's clothing and captures their voice clearly, even in noisy environments. This is often the best option for streeter interviews.
  • A shotgun mic: A solid middle-ground option that's more directional than a built-in mic, helping to isolate the speaker's voice from surrounding noise.

You don't need a professional studio setup, but even a modest investment in audio equipment will dramatically improve the quality of your final product. If your audience can't clearly hear what your interviewees are saying, even the most insightful answers lose their impact.

Why the Streeter Interview Deserves a Place in Your Content Strategy

The streeter interview works because it's authentic. In a content landscape saturated with polished brand messaging and scripted talking points, there's something refreshing and persuasive about real people sharing unfiltered opinions. It builds trust with your audience, surfaces genuine insights you might never have uncovered otherwise, and creates dynamic, engaging video content that stands out.

Whether you're a business owner looking to understand your customers, a marketer looking for fresh content ideas, or a creator searching for a new format to experiment with, the streeter interview is a versatile tool worth adding to your repertoire. Grab a camera, pick your questions carefully, head to a busy spot in your community, and let the public do the talking. You might be surprised by what you learn — and so will your audience.
