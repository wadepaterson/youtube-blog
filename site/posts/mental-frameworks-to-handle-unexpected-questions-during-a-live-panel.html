How to Handle Any Question on a Live Panel Without Freezing Up

By Wade Paterson

Picture this: you're sitting on stage as part of a panel discussion, the moderator turns to you with a question, and your mind goes completely blank. You can't formulate an answer, panic starts creeping in, and you find yourself staring out at the audience wondering what they must be thinking about your silence. If this scenario sounds familiar—or if the mere thought of it has kept you from ever accepting a panel invitation—you're not alone. The good news is that with a few reliable mental frameworks in your back pocket, you can walk into any panel discussion with genuine confidence.

First, Give Yourself Permission to Pause

Before we dive into frameworks, let's address the elephant in the room: silence is not the enemy. While you may be internally freaking out during a brief pause, the audience isn't. In fact, some of the most impactful thought leaders are the ones who take a deliberate moment to consider a question before responding. That pause signals thoughtfulness, not incompetence. So the next time your mind needs a beat to catch up, remember that the audience reads composure where you feel panic.

The PREP Framework: Your Go-To Structure for Any Question

If you remember only one thing from this article, make it this four-letter acronym: PREP. It stands for:

  • Point — State your main idea clearly and concisely.
  • Reason — Explain why you hold that position.
  • Example — Offer a real-life story or tangible illustration.
  • Point — Circle back to reinforce your original idea.

Here's what PREP looks like in action. Suppose a moderator asks, "What makes a great office culture?" Your response might sound like this:

"I believe a great office culture is shaped by intentional leadership. The reason is that culture goes far beyond a motto printed on a poster hanging on the wall—it's shaped by the daily actions a leader models. For example, I've had the chance to work with some outstanding ReMax broker owners across Western Canada. Many of them run weekly meetings, offer additional training, and host social events on Friday afternoons. Their agents genuinely want to show up because of the energy those leaders create. And that all comes back to the core idea: great culture is the product of intentional leadership."

Notice how the response isn't just a one-line answer. It takes the audience on a short journey—from claim, to reasoning, to a vivid real-world example, and back to the central point. This structure works because we as humans gravitate toward stories and tangible examples that help us envision how we might apply an idea to our own lives. A bare opinion, even a well-reasoned one, rarely lands with the same impact as one wrapped in a compelling illustration.

Acknowledge, Transition, Deliver: Navigating Tricky or Loaded Questions

Not every panel question is straightforward. Sometimes a question feels like a trap—whatever you say might alienate part of your audience or put you in an uncomfortable spotlight. For those moments, use the Acknowledge, Transition, Deliver framework.

  • Acknowledge — Show that you've heard and respect the question.
  • Transition — Pivot gracefully toward the terrain where you can add the most value.
  • Deliver — Share your expertise confidently on that reframed ground.

Imagine you're a financial advisor and a moderator asks, "How do you think current government economic policies are impacting investors?" That's a minefield. Your audience likely holds a wide spectrum of political beliefs, and taking a strong partisan stance could undermine your credibility with half the room. Here's how Acknowledge, Transition, Deliver handles it:

"There are certainly a lot of opinions on how the government is handling the current economic situation. What I like to focus on are strategies that work under any administration, because today's government could change at the next election. So I advise my clients on diversification, risk management, and long-term investment strategies with a proven track record. The best investors aren't getting caught up in short-term policy swings—they're looking through a much longer lens. And I believe that's a strategy almost anyone can deploy."

You've still provided genuine value. You've still addressed the question. But you haven't walked into the trap of injecting polarizing opinions where they don't serve you or your audience.

What You Do When You're Not Speaking Matters More Than You Think

Here's something most panelists overlook: your off-mic behavior is still on display. If you're part of a three-person panel, you might only be actively speaking for a third of the session. What you do during the other two-thirds shapes how the audience perceives your expertise and professionalism.

  • Listen actively to every question and answer — Moderators love to pivot unexpectedly with something like, "What do you think about what Susie just said?" If you weren't paying attention, it shows immediately.
  • Look for opportunities to add value — If another panelist touches on something you can build upon, respectfully offer to jump in. Whether you want to reinforce their point or share a different perspective, this kind of engagement elevates the entire discussion.
  • Maintain eye contact and approachable body language — Make eye contact with the moderator and the audience. Keep a genuine smile on your face. Many panelists default to a serious, almost stern resting expression without realizing it, and that energy radiates across the room.
  • Bring your personality — Humor, warmth, enthusiasm—everything you'd bring to a solo presentation belongs on the panel stage too. The audience wants to feel like they're part of an enjoyable, dynamic conversation, not a stiff Q&A.

Remember: even when the spotlight isn't directly on you, the audience is still watching. Stay engaged, stay present, and stay approachable throughout the entire session.

Confidence Comes from Preparation, Not Perfection

Paneling well isn't about having a perfect answer for every conceivable question. It's about having reliable structures that let you think on your feet with clarity and composure. Use PREP to give any answer depth and memorability. Use Acknowledge, Transition, Deliver to navigate questions that feel risky or loaded. And never underestimate the power of what you communicate when you're simply sitting on stage, listening.

With these mental frameworks in your toolkit, the next time a panel invitation lands in your inbox, you won't feel dread—you'll feel ready. The silence between a question and your answer isn't something to fear; it's the space where thoughtful, impactful responses are born.
