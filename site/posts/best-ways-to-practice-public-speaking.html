7 Powerful Ways to Practice Public Speaking and Level Up Your Skills

By Wade Paterson

Public speaking is quickly becoming one of the most valuable skill sets for professional success. Whether you've set it as a personal goal or you simply want to communicate with more confidence, the truth is that becoming a better speaker requires deliberate, consistent practice. The good news? There are proven strategies you can start using right now to sharpen your abilities — no stage required. Here are seven practical ways to take your public speaking skills to the next level.

1. Join a Toastmasters Club

If you haven't tried Toastmasters, it remains one of the single best ways to improve your public speaking skills. As an international program, Toastmasters clubs exist all over the world — and even if there isn't one in your area, virtual clubs make it easy to participate from anywhere.

The real power of Toastmasters lies in consistent repetition. Practicing week after week builds your confidence and sharpens your delivery over time. Think of it like going to the gym: telling someone you attended Toastmasters ten years ago is like saying you worked out ten years ago. If you don't maintain the habit, the skills deteriorate. Public speaking is something you have to work on regularly, and Toastmasters provides the ideal structure to do exactly that.

2. Don't Wait — Start Working on a Speech Now

Too many aspiring speakers sit around waiting for someone to invite them to deliver a speech. Stop waiting and start preparing one today.

Here's a story that illustrates the point: In 11th-grade English class, a teacher handed each student a blank sheet of paper and gave a simple assignment — write whatever you want. Some students scribbled a single word and handed it in. But for those who took it as an invitation to be creative, it became a transformative exercise. That blank page was permission to do their best work.

Consider this your permission. Start building a speech right now. Here are a few ideas to get you going:

  • At work: Prepare a presentation on a topic you're knowledgeable about or an idea you could teach your colleagues.
  • At a friend's wedding: Even if you're not in the wedding party, draft a speech you could give. Some weddings open the microphone to guests — and you'll be ready.
  • For any occasion: Always have a speech you're actively working on. The process of building it out is practice in itself.

3. Eliminate Your Filler Words

Filler words — um, uh, so, like, but — are the sounds and phrases we inject between sentences that add no value to what we're saying. We use them because silence can feel uncomfortable. But here's what most speakers don't realize: pauses aren't distracting for the audience. In fact, a well-placed pause between sentences is powerful and gives your words room to land.

Eliminating filler words is one of the fastest ways to level up as a speaker. Start by identifying which fillers you rely on most. Record yourself speaking and watch the footage back. Ask trusted friends or colleagues to flag your filler words after a presentation. If you're in Toastmasters, the program has built-in roles designed to help with exactly this. Once you know your tendencies, you can actively work to replace those fillers with confident, purposeful silence.

4. Take a 100-Day Speaking Challenge

One of the most exciting trends in the speaking space is the 100-day speaking challenge. The concept is simple: commit to recording and posting a video every single day for 100 consecutive days. The results are remarkable — the difference between Day 1 and Day 100 is often staggering.

If 100 days feels daunting, start with a three-month challenge. For a full quarter, practice creating video content daily and actually post it on a social media platform of your choice. Don't limit yourself to disappearing stories — publish it on your main accounts. Putting your content out into the world adds a layer of accountability that accelerates growth. By the end of those three months, you'll notice a dramatic improvement in both your skills and your comfort in front of a camera.

5. Review Your Recordings Strategically

Recording yourself is only half the equation. How you review that footage matters just as much. Try this three-pass approach:

  • First pass — watch without sound: Focus entirely on your body language. Do you sway back and forth? Are your hands stuffed in your pockets or clasped behind your back? Do you project nervous energy? Muting the audio forces you to see habits you'd otherwise miss.
  • Second pass — listen without watching: Put the phone away, turn up the volume, and simply listen. This is where you'll catch filler words, moments where your voice lacks confidence, or sections where you ramble. Isolating the audio lets you focus purely on vocal delivery.
  • Third pass — watch normally: Finally, watch the recording as a viewer would, combining body language and sound together to get the full picture.

By isolating body language and vocal variety before combining them, you gain a much clearer understanding of where you need to improve.

6. Educate Yourself by Following the Experts

One of the simplest ways to improve is to learn from people who have already mastered the craft. Immerse yourself in content from communication experts who can offer fresh insights and actionable techniques. A few recommendations:

  • Vanessa Van Edwards: An expert in communication skills with a wealth of ideas to help you refine your public speaking abilities.
  • Vinh Giang: Especially brilliant when it comes to vocal variety, with millions of followers and deep expertise in the communication space.

Consuming educational content about speaking keeps the skill top-of-mind and exposes you to strategies you might never discover on your own.

7. Invest in Accelerated Learning

If you're serious about making rapid progress, consider investing in a structured course or accelerator program. While there is an abundance of free content available online, a dedicated program with guided instruction, feedback, and accountability can compress months of self-directed learning into just a few weeks. The financial investment often serves as additional motivation to show up fully and do the work.

Look for programs that run over a defined period — three weeks to a few months — and that offer hands-on practice rather than passive lectures. The combination of expert guidance and intensive repetition is one of the quickest paths to measurable improvement.

Conclusion

Becoming a confident, compelling public speaker isn't about waiting for the perfect moment or being born with natural talent. It's about showing up consistently and practicing with intention. Whether you join Toastmasters, challenge yourself to post daily videos, study the experts, or simply start writing a speech today, every step you take brings you closer to the speaker you want to become. The skills you build now will pay dividends across every area of your professional and personal life — so pick one of these strategies and start today.
