Why You Should Launch a Podcast or YouTube Interview Series (And Why Attention Is the Real Currency)

By Wade Paterson

In an age where capturing even 20 seconds of someone's attention feels like a Herculean task, podcasting has quietly become one of the most powerful communication tools available. Whether you're a business owner, a thought leader, or simply someone with a story to tell, launching a podcast or interview series could be one of the smartest moves you make. But before diving into equipment lists and interview techniques, there's a fundamental question worth answering first: why would you want to do this in the first place?

From Skeptic to Convert: A Personal Podcasting Origin Story

Not everyone starts out as a podcast enthusiast. In fact, plenty of people — even those deeply passionate about communication — spend years ignoring the medium entirely. That was exactly the case for Wade Patterson, a communication specialist who spent years hearing friends rave about their favourite shows without ever understanding the hype.

The turning point came during a work trip. Flying from Kelowna, British Columbia, to Toronto, Wade found himself endlessly skipping through thousands of songs on his phone. Nothing satisfied him. He'd simply listened to everything too many times. During his layover, he posted a simple question on Facebook: "What are some great business-related podcasts you enjoy?"

The response was overwhelming. He downloaded several recommendations and pressed play on the next leg of his flight to Washington. The effect was immediate — an aha moment. Instead of killing time with background music, he was suddenly learning. He was absorbing insights on business, communication, and a range of fascinating topics, all in an entertaining format that felt effortless.

Replacing Dead Time with Growth

When Wade returned home, the habit stuck. Podcasts replaced music during his morning commute. They became his gym companion. Every moment that had previously been filled with passive listening was now an opportunity for education and inspiration.

This is one of podcasting's quiet superpowers: it fits into the cracks of daily life. You don't need to set aside dedicated time the way you would for reading a book or watching a video. Podcasts layer seamlessly on top of activities you're already doing — driving, exercising, cooking, walking the dog. For someone who describes themselves as a slow reader, this was transformative. Two years of consistent listening later, the volume of knowledge absorbed was staggering.

The Real Why: Podcasts Capture Attention Like Nothing Else

Here's the core insight, and it's a powerful one: podcasts allow you to capture someone's attention in a way that almost no other medium can.

Think about how ruthlessly we guard our time online. A 20-second video on social media? Many of us scroll right past it, unwilling to invest even that small commitment. A 10-minute YouTube video? Viewers skip ahead, multitask, or abandon it halfway through. Attention is one of the most coveted and most difficult things to earn in today's media landscape.

Yet somehow, podcasts have circumvented this rule entirely. People willingly listen to episodes that are 20 minutes, 40 minutes, or even three hours long — and they listen from start to finish. The key is understanding how that listening happens:

  • 15 minutes in the car on the way to work
  • An hour at the gym during a workout
  • 30 minutes at home while cooking dinner on a smart speaker

Bit by bit, episode by episode, listeners absorb enormous amounts of content throughout their week. They develop a genuine relationship with the host's voice, perspective, and ideas. That kind of sustained, voluntary attention is extraordinarily rare — and extraordinarily valuable.

What This Means for You as a Creator

If you can produce a podcast with decent audio quality and genuinely interesting content, you have real potential to build something meaningful. You're not just growing an audience — you're growing an audience that spends significant time engaging with what you have to say. That depth of connection is something a tweet, an Instagram post, or even a short-form video simply cannot replicate.

And here's the good news: you don't have to choose between a podcast and a YouTube channel. Many creators launch a YouTube interview series and release the audio as a podcast byproduct — or vice versa. The skills, strategies, and principles are largely the same. Whether your content lives on Apple Podcasts, Spotify, YouTube, or all of the above, the fundamentals of launching well remain consistent.

The Rogan Challenge: Give It a Real Shot

One concept worth embracing is what Wade calls "The Rogan" — a challenge to not just launch a podcast, but to commit to publishing at least five episodes. Why five? Because you can't judge the experience from a single episode. You need a handful of recordings under your belt to find your rhythm, refine your process, and genuinely evaluate whether podcasting delivers value for you and your audience. One episode is a test. Five episodes is a real attempt.

Ready to Take the Leap?

Launching a podcast or interview series might feel daunting, but the barrier to entry has never been lower, and the potential rewards — in audience growth, personal branding, learning, and connection — have never been greater. The "why" is clear: podcasting gives you access to the scarcest resource in modern media, your audience's sustained attention. If you have something worth saying, there are people out there ready to listen — on their commute, at the gym, and in their kitchen. The question isn't really why you should start a podcast. It's why haven't you started one yet?
