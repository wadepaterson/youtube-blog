How to Be an Effective Panelist: A Complete Guide to Shining on Any Panel

By Wade Paterson

If you've ever attended a conference, you've probably noticed something interesting: the majority of sessions aren't solo keynote speeches — they're panel conversations. Yet most public speaking training focuses almost exclusively on the one-to-many format, where a single speaker commands the stage alone. Panel discussions are a fundamentally different beast. You're sharing the spotlight with other experts, you're often seated, and the dynamic is more conversational than performative. So how do you stand out and deliver real value when you're one voice among several? Here's everything you need to know about being a great panelist.

Recognize That Panels Are a Different Format

The first thing to understand is that a panel conversation is structurally different from a solo speaking engagement. You're sharing the stage with other people, and you're almost certainly going to be seated. That might sound like a minor detail, but it has significant implications. You can't walk around the stage, use sweeping gestures, or command the room with your physical presence the way a keynote speaker can. Your body language toolkit is immediately reduced to what you can do from a chair — primarily your arms, hands, and facial expressions.

Recognizing these constraints upfront allows you to prepare accordingly and adjust your approach so you can still deliver a memorable, engaging performance within the format.

Prepare Thoroughly — But Not Rigidly

If you've been invited to sit on a panel, congratulations — it's a genuine honour. You've likely been selected because you bring real expertise to the subject matter. But expertise alone isn't enough; preparedness is what separates good panelists from great ones.

A strong panel moderator will often set up a pre-interview or send questions in advance. But don't wait passively for that to happen. If you haven't heard from the moderator, take the initiative:

  • Reach out and ask about the key themes and likely questions.
  • Find out logistical details — what time is the panel, how long will it run, and how early should you arrive?
  • Visit the room or venue beforehand if possible to get comfortable with the environment.
  • Research your co-panelists and the moderator so you understand their backgrounds and areas of expertise.

That last point is especially valuable. A panel is a team effort, and the best panels are the ones where every participant shines. When you know a bit about your fellow panelists, you can craft natural transitions between answers, acknowledge their expertise, and build on what they've said rather than simply waiting for your turn to talk.

Don't Script Your Answers Word for Word

Once you have a sense of the questions, resist the urge to write out polished, word-for-word responses. It's fine to jot down notes, key themes, or bullet points you want to hit. But reading from a script on stage is one of the fastest ways to lose an audience.

The entire value of a live panel is authenticity. People are there to hear real-time insights from knowledgeable experts, not rehearsed paragraphs delivered from a sheet of paper.

Here's a real-world example that drives this point home. The first time I was invited to appear on CBC Radio, I spent the entire night before writing out detailed responses to every question I thought I might be asked. When I arrived at the studio, the interviewer asked to see my notes. After I explained what I'd written, he crumpled up the paper and threw it away. "Perfect," he said. "You don't need that."

He was absolutely right. Whether you're on radio, on stage, or on a panel, audiences can instantly tell the difference between someone reading a prepared response and someone speaking from genuine knowledge. Practice your answers, know your material inside and out — but leave the script behind.

Engage in Active Listening

This is where many panelists fall short. It's tempting to spend your time mentally rehearsing your next answer while someone else is speaking. But doing so means you miss the most valuable opportunities a panel format offers.

Active listening is what creates those magical, unscripted moments — the witty observation, the perfect follow-up, the comment that ties two different perspectives together in a way nobody expected. These are the moments that make you memorable.

There are also practical reasons to stay present. If you're not listening, you might repeat something a co-panelist has already said, which makes you look unprepared. Or you might miss a chance to build on a point in a way that adds genuine depth to the conversation.

Active listening extends to the audience as well. If you notice people crossing their arms, checking their phones, or generally disengaging, that's a signal. Skilled panelists will seize that moment to re-engage the room — ask the audience a direct question, invite a show of hands, or shift the energy in some way. Once you've pulled them back in, whatever you say next is far more likely to resonate.

Be Ready for Curveballs

No matter how well you prepare, expect the unexpected. This is one of the realities of panel discussions that catches many people off guard.

The moderator might steer the conversation in a completely different direction than what was discussed beforehand. They're the quarterback of the session, and it's their prerogative to adapt based on how the discussion is unfolding. You can't tell the moderator not to ask you something — you simply have to respond.

Audience Q&A adds another layer of unpredictability. These are questions you absolutely cannot prepare for, which makes your ability to think on your feet essential. The combination of deep subject-matter knowledge and the flexibility to pivot is what separates a panelist who merely participates from one who truly commands the room.

Maximize Your Body Language From a Seated Position

Since you'll likely be seated, you need to overcompensate with the tools you do have. Bring your hands up. Use them expressively to emphasize key points. Lean in when you're making an important statement. Make eye contact not just with the moderator, but with the audience and your co-panelists.

Just because you can't move around the stage doesn't mean your body language has to be flat. The panelists who understand this distinction are the ones who hold attention even from a chair.

Apply the Fundamentals of Great Public Speaking

Everything you know about effective public speaking still applies in a panel setting — it just looks slightly different. Keep these fundamentals in mind:

  • Structure your answers: Even in a conversational format, aim for a clear opening comment, a substantive middle, and a concise conclusion.
  • Use vocal variety: Vary your tone, pace, and volume to keep the audience engaged, just as you would in a solo speech.
  • Be mindful of time: Pay attention to how long your co-panelists are speaking. If everyone else is giving one-to-two-minute answers and you're delivering four-minute monologues, you'll come across as dominating the stage.
  • Complement, don't compete: Use active listening to comment on what others have said and show how your perspective adds to or builds upon their points. Great panelists make the whole conversation better, not just their own segments.

Enjoy the Moment

Being invited to join a panel is a recognition of your expertise and your value as a communicator. It's not something that happens every day, and you've earned it. So while preparation and technique matter enormously, don't forget to actually enjoy the process. The panelists who are visibly engaged, enthusiastic, and present are the ones audiences remember long after the session ends.

Panel discussions are one of the most dynamic and rewarding formats in public speaking. They demand a unique blend of preparation and spontaneity, expertise and humility, confidence and collaboration. Master these skills — prepare thoroughly, listen actively, stay flexible, use your body language wisely, and respect both your co-panelists and your audience — and you won't just be a good panelist. You'll be the one everyone talks about after the conference is over.
